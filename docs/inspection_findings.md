|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|

This will be discussed in detail at the Task-B design point, but flagging it

## 2. Coordinate-system findings

### 2.1 World frame: Z is the vertical axis, but its direction is data-dependent

| Scene | Pointcloud Z range (m) | Pose Z range (m) |
|---|---|---|
| BasicHouse | `[-3.62, +0.30]` | `[-1.61, -0.51]` |
| synagoge | `[-16.05, +0.30]` | `[-9.41, -1.10]` |

Z is the smallest-span axis in both scenes (BasicHouse: 3.9 m; synagoge:
16.3 m), confirming Z is vertical. But which direction is "up" is **not**
inferable from the bounds alone — it depends on Revit/Blender export
convention, which differs across IFC sources.

**The robust answer comes from the `IfcSlab` data** (see §3 below for IFC
recovery). For BasicHouse:

```
Floor:Bjälklag        z = [ 0.00,  0.30]   ← named "Floor" slab
Basic Roof            z = [-3.62, -2.25]   ← named "Roof" slab
Camera trajectory     z ∈ [-1.61, -0.51]   ← between them
```

So the building's floor surface is at z ≈ 0 (high Z), the roof at z ≈ -3.6
(low Z), and "up in real-world" maps to "−Z direction in this world frame".
This is the opposite of the more common convention.

**Practical consequence for code:** never hardcode "the floor is at the
minimum Z" or vice-versa. Use `IfcSlab` Z extents (or BEV Z-density peaks)
to identify horizontal levels, and define rooms as the *interval between
adjacent slab levels* — orientation-independent. This is the approach used
in `src/rgbdsg/ifc/rooms_bev.py`.

### 2.2 ⚠ Camera frame: assumed OpenGL (Blender) — needs back-projection check

`render_summary.json` confirms data was rendered in Blender 4.0.2. Blender's
camera convention is **OpenGL**: +X right, +Y up, **−Z forward** (camera looks
down −Z in its own frame). Computer-vision pinhole convention is the opposite:
+X right, +Y down, **+Z forward**.

This matters for back-projection. Two equally plausible conventions for the
depth values:

1. Depth = positive distance along camera +Z (CV convention) → standard formula
   `X_cam = (u - cx) * d / fx`, `Y_cam = (v - cy) * d / fy`, `Z_cam = d`.
2. Depth = positive distance along camera −Z (Blender Z-distance → linear depth)
   → need to flip Y and Z signs, or premultiply pose by `diag(1, −1, −1, 1)`.

**This is the single most likely source of a silent coordinate bug.** Step 2's
back-projection-and-overlay test (this is what the user explicitly called out)
will resolve it.

### 2.3 Pose convention: `world-from-camera` (T_wc)

Both `pose/poses.txt` and `frames.json` agree to ~5 nm on translation and
~4×10⁻⁷ on rotation. The 4×4 matrix's translation column equals the camera
position in world coords (matches the in-scene trajectory bounds), so the
matrix is `T_wc` — point-in-camera ↦ point-in-world. To project world→pixel,
invert: `T_cw = T_wc⁻¹`.

Quaternion in `frames.json` is **xyzw order** (Hamilton). Converting it to a
rotation matrix matches `poses.txt` perfectly. Either source is fine.

### 2.4 Pose ↔ pointcloud bbox sanity

For both scenes, the entire camera trajectory lies inside the pointcloud's
axis-aligned bounding box (with negative axis overshoot — i.e. trajectory is
strictly *interior*). This is necessary but not sufficient for full alignment;
step 2 verifies it geometrically.

## 4. Depth encoding — both formats explained

**Both EXR and PNG16 are dense — they have no zero / no-data sentinel.**
"No geometry hit" pixels are encoded at the **high end**:

| Format | Saturation value | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `depth_exr/*.exr` (fp16) | `65504.0` (fp16 max) | ray escaped / no hit |
| `depth_png16/*.png` (uint16) | `65535` (uint16 max) | same |

`pct_invalid` is therefore **not** `(depth == 0).mean()` — it is
`(depth >= 0.99 * far_m).mean()`. Some frames have 0% saturation, others (like
synagoge frame 0, looking out a window) have 50%+. A robust pipeline must mask
on the high end.

### 4.1 PNG16 ↔ EXR scale

Theoretical: `png = depth_m × (65535 / far_m) = depth_m × 655.35`.

Empirical (median across non-saturated frames in both scenes):

| Scene | Estimated PNG/m | Theory | Match within 2%? |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| BasicHouse | 649.55 | 655.35 | ✓ |
| synagoge | 649.55 | 655.35 | ✓ |

Consistent ~0.9% under-shoot — most likely the encoder used `far ≈ 100.9 m` or
applies a half-LSB rounding bias. Not worth chasing; we'll use EXR directly.

### 4.2 Recommendation: use EXR, ignore PNG

EXR is float, lossless within fp16, and already in meters. PNG is quantized
(`Δ = 1/649.5 ≈ 1.54 mm`), saturates at 100 m, and offers no advantage on
modern hardware. The PNG copy is provided for tooling that can't read EXR.

0.0025` (sub-millimeter) — the camera grazes a wall on at least one frame. We
should expect occasional bad geometry (degenerate depth, near-clipping artifacts).

## 7. Step-2 results — open questions resolved

All three open questions resolved by `scripts/verify_pose.py`, which back-
projects depth under four candidate conventions and scores by median nearest-
neighbour distance to `scene.ply`. Full writeup in `docs/coordinates.md`.

| Question | Resolved answer | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Camera-axis convention | **OpenGL / Blender** (+Y up, −Z forward) | `gl_z` median NN 41 mm vs `cv_z` 292 mm on BasicHouse |
| Depth interpretation | **Planar Z-distance** (not Euclidean ray) | `gl_z` 41 mm vs `gl_ray` 125 mm |
| Quaternion handedness | Standard Hamilton xyzw, no flip | `quat_xyzw_to_R(frames.json) ≈ poses.txt[:3,:3]` to 4 × 10⁻⁷ |

Both scenes give the same winning convention. Pose+depth+intrinsics now form
a self-consistent metric pipeline; we can write `src/geometry/` against this.
